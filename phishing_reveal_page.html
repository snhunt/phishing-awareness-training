🚩 The Red Flags You Should Have Spotted

1

Urgent/Threatening Language

Phishing emails create panic to bypass your critical thinking. Words like "URGENT," "expires tonight," "account suspended," and "immediate action required" are designed to make you act without thinking.

"This link expires in 2 hours. Failure to verify will result in your account being suspended."
2

Generic Greeting

Legitimate institutions usually address you by name. Phishing emails use generic greetings like "Dear Student," "Dear Customer," or "Dear User" because they're sent to thousands of people.

"Dear Student," (instead of "Dear [Your Name],")
3

Suspicious Sender Email

Always check the sender's email address carefully. Phishers use addresses that look similar to real ones but have slight differences or completely different domains.

Real: it-support@yourschool.edu
Fake: it-support@yourschool-security.com
4

Fake Links (Hover Before Clicking!)

Before clicking any link, hover over it to see the actual URL. Phishing links often lead to fake websites with URLs that look almost right but have subtle misspellings or extra words.

Real: portal.yourschool.edu
Fake: portal-yourschool.com or yourschool-portal.net
5

Requests for Login Credentials

Legitimate IT departments will NEVER ask you to enter your password via email link. They'll ask you to go directly to the official website and log in normally.

Any email asking you to "verify your account" by entering your password is suspicious.
6

Artificial Deadlines

Creating a false sense of urgency with countdown timers or "expires in X hours" is a classic tactic to prevent you from thinking critically or verifying the request.

Countdown timers, "expires in 2 hours," "24-hour deadline"

⚠️ What Happens in Real Phishing Attacks?

If this had been a real phishing attack, here's what criminals could do with your information:

🔓

Account Takeover

Access your school portal, change grades, view personal records, or lock you out of your own account.

💳

Financial Theft

If you reuse passwords, they could access your bank accounts, shopping sites, or payment apps.

🎭

Identity Theft

Use your personal information to open credit cards, apply for loans, or commit fraud in your name.

📧

Spread Malware

Use your compromised account to send phishing emails to your contacts, who trust messages from you.

📱

Social Engineering

Gather information about you to craft more convincing attacks against you or your family.

🔒

Ransomware

Deploy malware that encrypts your files and demands payment to restore access.

🛡️ How to Protect Yourself

  • ✓

    STOP and THINK: When you feel urgency or panic, take a breath. Real emergencies don't come via email with countdown timers.

  • ✓

    Verify Independently: Don't use contact info from the suspicious email. Look up the official website or phone number separately.

  • ✓

    Hover Before Clicking: Always hover over links to see the actual URL before clicking. On mobile, long-press to preview.

  • ✓

    Check the Sender: Look carefully at the email address, not just the display name. Watch for misspellings or odd domains.

  • ✓

    Use Unique Passwords: Never reuse passwords across sites. Use a password manager to generate and store strong, unique passwords.

  • ✓

    Enable 2FA: Two-factor authentication adds an extra layer of security even if your password is stolen.

  • ✓

    When in Doubt, Ask: Forward suspicious emails to your IT department or ask a teacher before clicking anything.
